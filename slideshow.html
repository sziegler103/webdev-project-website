Info

These are all photographs I took at some point that I thought were interesting enough to display here. They were taken with a Canon PowerShot SX530 HS. It has up to 50x optical zoom and wireless/NFC capabilities integrated into it. Besides that, it's not that much different than a cheap(er) point-and-shoot camera. For example, it doesn't have a view finder, only a digital display. On a spectrum of camera grades, it would sit in the middle of point-and-shoot style cameras and heavy-weight DSLR (Digital Single-Lens Reflex) cameras. Cameras that sit in that range are called "bridge" cameras, because they bridge the gap between the two extremes. The SX530 is lacking when it comes to its aperture range and ISO settings, but its manual mode still allows for some creative control when necessary. The extreme amount of zoom the camera is capable of creates opportunities for photographs which would otherwise require very expensive telescopic lenses to capture.

As for the pictures themselves, they were taken at the highest quality setting allowable by the camera. Their lighting was slightly adjusted and the color saturation was somewhat boosted on a few of them. To prepare them for the web, they were downscaled to 600x450, and compressed with a 95% JPEG quality setting. As a note about the caption of the last picture, the image sensor of any digital camera can be damaged by the sun if exposed for even seconds, as the lens of the camera focuses the sunlight into a tight beam, which is then directed at the Charged Couple Device or Complementary Metal-Oxide Semiconductor. Similarly to anything on the other end of a properly adjusted magnifying glass on a sunny day, this is not good for the internals of your camera. However, SLR-type cameras redirect all light through their viewfinders, only exposing their film or image sensor for however long the shutter speed is set to open. So while one should avoid looking into an SLR's viewfinder at the sun, pictures "fired from the hip" can still be taken without too much risk to the camera itself.
